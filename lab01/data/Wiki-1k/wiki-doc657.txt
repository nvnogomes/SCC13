EON Productions

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

   Jump to: [6]navigation, [7]search

   EON Productions is a [8]film production company known for producing the
   [9]James Bond [10]film series. The company is based in London's
   Piccadilly and also operates from [11]Pinewood Studios in the
   [12]United Kingdom. It is a subsidiary of [13]Danjaq LLC, the
   [14]holding company responsible for the [15]copyright and
   [16]trademarks to the Bond characters and elements on screen.

[[23]edit] Bond films

          Main article: [24]James Bond (film series)

   EON was started by [25]film producers [26]Albert R. Broccoli and
   [27]Harry Saltzman in [28]1961. In [29]1975 after nine Bond films,
   Harry Saltzman sold his shares of Danjaq to [30]United Artists (the
   then-current Bond series distributor). Although Albert R. Broccoli died
   in [31]1996, EON Productions is still owned by the Broccoli family,
   specifically Albert R. Broccoli's daughter, [32]Barbara Broccoli, and
   his stepson, [33]Michael G. Wilson, who are the current producers of
   the James Bond films. Every EON-produced James Bond film's opening
   credits start with Albert R. Broccoli's name (initially as "Albert R.
   Broccoli presents" and "Albert R. Broccoli's EON Productions present"
   after his death).

   The trademarks for the film properties (beginning with [34]Dr. No) are
   copyrighted by Danjaq and [35]United Artists Corporation. The latter
   were bought by [36]Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer in 1981: Casino Royale and
   Quantum of Solace (2006, 2008) were co-distributed with [37]Columbia
   Pictures.

   These EON productions are commonly regarded as "official" James Bond
   films, whereas the 1954 television adaptation of [38]Casino Royale, the
   1967 film spoof [39]Casino Royale and [40]Sean Connery's 1983 film
   [41]Never Say Never Again are regarded as "unofficial", a misleading
   term in that all three were official, legal adaptations; the term
   merely indicates that they were not produced by EON and do not form
   part of this company's canon (this also means that they lack certain
   familiar elements such as the [42]James Bond Theme music and the
   Gunbarrel sequence).^[43][1]^[44][2]

[[91]edit] Other productions

   Since their first film, [92]Dr. No in [93]1962, there has only been one
   film made by EON Productions that wasn't a part of the Bond series:
   [94]Call Me Bwana, starring [95]Bob Hope. (Though Saltzman and Broccoli
   produced other films separately and jointly including the film
   [96]Chitty Chitty Bang Bang, also based on a book by [97]Ian Fleming.)

   In 2008, EON signed a deal with [98]Columbia Pictures to develop
   fifteen thrillers and family films outside the Bond franchise, with
   budgets of up to [99]US$80 million ([100]GBP40 million). The company
   hopes the move will allow more British writers to establish themselves
   in the United States.^[101][3]

[[102]edit] Games

   In [103]2000, EON productions served a [104]cease-and-desist letter to
   [105]Cheapass Games to stop them from using the name "Mr. Bond" in the
   title of their game [106]Before I Kill You, Mr. Bond. In [107]2004, the
   game was reissued under the title James Ernest's Totally Renamed Spy
   Game.

   In [108]2004 [109]Electronic Arts developed a [110]video game with the
   James Bond [111]licence titled [112]Everything or Nothing, which was
   thought to be named after the production company. Up until his death,
   Albert R. Broccoli had always denied that "EON" stood for anything
   specifically.
