Dory (fish)

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

   Jump to: [6]navigation, [7]search

   The [8]common name dory (from the [9]Middle English dorre, from the
   [10]Middle French doree, lit. "gilded one") is shared (officially and
   colloquially) by members of several different [11]families of
   large-eyed, silvery, deep-bodied, laterally compressed, and roughly
   discoid marine [12]fish. As well as resembling each other, dories are
   also similar in habit: most are deep-sea and [13]demersal.
   Additionally, many species support commercial [14]fisheries and are
   considered excellent [15]food fish. Most dory families belong to the
   [16]order [17]Zeiformes, suborder Zeioidei:
     * The "true dories", family [18]Zeidae (five species, including the
       well-known [19]John dory)
     * The [20]zeniontids, family [21]Zenionidae or Zeniontidae (seven
       species)
     * The "Australian dories", family [22]Cyttidae (three species all
       within the [23]genus [24]Cyttus)
     * The [25]oreos, family [26]Oreosomatidae (ten species)
     * The [27]parazen family, [28]Parazenidae (four species, including
       the [29]rosy dory)

   Additionally, several species of [30]spinyfin (family [31]Diretmidae,
   order [32]Beryciformes) have been given the name dory by
   [33]fishmongers, presumably to make the fish more marketable.

   In parts of Southeast Asia, fillets of [34]Pangasius sp. catfishes are
   referred to as cream dory.

[[35]edit] In popular culture

   In the [36]Pixar film [37]Finding Nemo, [38]Dory is the name of a
   [39]regal tang fish, which is not a dory.
