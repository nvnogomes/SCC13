   Gluconic acid is an [27]organic compound with [28]molecular formula
   C[6]H[12]O[7] and condensed [29]structural [30]formula
   HOCH[2](CHOH)[4]COOH. In aqueous solution at neutral [31]pH, gluconic
   acid forms the gluconate ion. The [32]salts of gluconic acid are known
   as "gluconates". Gluconic acid and gluconate salts occur widely in
   nature because such species arise from the [33]oxidation of
   [34]glucose. Gluconate [35]esters can also be formed, such as
   [36]quinine gluconate which is used for [37]intramuscular injection in
   the treatment of [38]malaria.

[[43]edit] Chemical structure

   The [44]chemical structure of gluconic acid consists of a six-carbon
   chain with five [45]hydroxyl groups terminating in a carboxylic acid
   group. In aqueous solution, gluconic acid exists in equilibrium with
   the cyclic ester [46]Glucono delta lactone.

[[47]edit] Occurrence and uses

   Gluconic acid occurs naturally in [48]fruit, [49]honey, [50]kombucha
   tea, and [51]wine. As a [52]food additive (E574^[53][1]), it is an
   [54]acidity regulator. It is also used in cleaning products where it
   dissolves mineral deposits especially in alkaline solution. The
   gluconate anion [55]chelates [56]Ca^2+, [57]Fe^2+, [58]Al^3+, and other
   heavy metals. [59]Calcium gluconate, in the form of a gel, is used to
   treat burns from [60]hydrofluoric acid.^[61][2]^[62][3]
