   Donn Fulton Eisele ([18]June 23, [19]1930 [20]December 2, [21]1987),
   born in [22]Columbus, Ohio, was a test pilot in the [23]USAF and a
   [24]NASA astronaut.

[[33]edit] Education

   Eisele graduated in 1948 from West High School in Columbus, Ohio. He
   received a Bachelor of Science degree from the [34]United States Naval
   Academy in 1952 and a Master of Science degree in Astronautics in 1960
   from the [35]Air Force Institute of Technology, [36]Wright-Patterson
   AFB, Ohio.

[[37]edit] Organizations

   Eisele was a member of [38]Tau Beta Pi, the National Engineering
   Society, and a [39]Freemason, belonging to Turner Lodge #732 in
   Columbus, Ohio.

[[40]edit] Special honors

   Eisele was an [41]Eagle Scout. Among the honors he received during his
   military and NASA career were the NASA Exceptional Service Medal, the
   Air Force Senior Pilot Astronaut Wings, and the Air Force
   [42]Distinguished Flying Cross. He was a co-recipient of the [43]AIAA
   1969 Haley Astronautics Award and was presented the National Academy of
   Television Arts and Sciences Special Trustees Award in 1969.

[[44]edit] Experience

   Although he graduated from the United States Naval Academy, Eisele
   chose a commission in the United States Air Force. He graduated from
   the Air Force Aerospace Research Pilot School at [45]Edwards AFB,
   California.

   Eisele was a project engineer and experimental test pilot at the Air
   Force Special Weapons Center at [46]Kirtland AFB, New Mexico. He flew
   experimental test flights in support of special weapons development
   programs.

   He logged more than 4,200 hours flying time 3,600 of those hours in jet
   aircraft.

[[47]edit] NASA

   Eisele was one of the third group of astronauts selected by NASA in
   October 1963.
   The Apollo 7 crew: Donn Eisele(l.), Wally Schirra (c.), and Walter
   Cunningham (r.)

   On [48]October 11, [49]1968, he occupied the command module pilot seat
   for the eleven-day flight of Apollo VII the first manned flight test of
   the third generation United States spacecraft. With spacecraft
   commander [50]Walter M. Schirra, Jr. and lunar module pilot [51]Walter
   Cunningham, Eisele participated in and executed maneuvers enabling the
   crew to perform exercises in transposition and docking and lunar orbit
   rendezvous with the S-IVB stage of their [52]Saturn IB launch vehicle.
   They completed eight successful test and maneuvering ignitions of the
   service module propulsion engine, They also measured the accuracy of
   performance of all spacecraft systems, and provided the first effective
   television transmissions of onboard crew activities.

   [53]Apollo 7 was placed in an Earth-orbit with an apogee of 153.5
   [54]nautical miles (284.3 kilometers) and perigee of 122.6 nautical
   miles (227.1 km). The 260-hour, four-and-a-half million mile (7.25 Gm)
   shakedown flight was successfully concluded on [55]October 22,
   [56]1968, with splashdown occurring in the [57]Atlantic, eight miles
   (15 km) from the carrier [58]Essex (only three-tenths of a mile or 0.6
   km from the originally predicted aiming point). Eisele logged 260 hours
   in space.

   Eisele also served as backup command module pilot for the 1969
   [59]Apollo 10 flight. Eisele resigned from the Astronaut Office in 1970
   and became technical assistant for manned spaceflight at the NASA
   [60]Langley Research Center, a position he occupied until retiring from
   both NASA and the Air Force in 1972.

[[61]edit] Post-NASA career

   In July 1972, Eisele became Country Director of the U.S. [62]Peace
   Corps in [63]Thailand. Upon returning from Thailand, he became Sales
   Manager for the Marion Power Shovel Company, a division of [64]Dresser
   Industries. Eisele then handled private and corporate accounts for the
   investment firm of Oppenheimer & Company.

   Eisele died at the age of 57 of a [65]heart attack while on a business
   trip to [66]Tokyo, Japan, where he was to attend the opening of a new
   [67]Space Camp patterned on the one at the [68]U.S. Space & Rocket
   Center in [69]Huntsville, Alabama. He was survived by his wife Susan
   and their two children. Eisele also had four children from a previous
   marriage. Eisele was cremated in Japan, and his ashes buried in
   [70]Arlington National Cemetery.

   A family-approved account of Donn Eisele's life appears in the 2007
   book [71]In the Shadow of the Moon.

   In the 1998 miniseries [72]From the Earth to the Moon, Eisele was
   played by [73]John Mese.

   In 2008, NASA posthumously awarded Eisele the [74]NASA Distinguished
   Service Medal for his Apollo 7 mission.^[75][1]
