Double harmonic scale

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

   Jump to: [6]navigation, [7]search

   In [8]music, the double harmonic major scale^[9][1] is a [10]scale
   whose [11]gaps may evoke "exotic" music to Western listeners. This is
   also known as the Gypsy, Arabic and ^[12][1]^[13][2] the Byzantine
   scale.

   The sequence of [14]steps comprising the double harmonic scale is:
     * [15]half [16]minor third half [17]whole half [18]minor third half.

   Or, in relation to the [19]tonic note:
     * flat 2nd, major 3rd, perfect 4th and 5th, minor 6th, 7th.

   However, this scale is commonly represented with the first and last
   half step each being represented as a [20]quarter tone. The non-quarter
   tone form is identical to the Indian [21]Thaat named Bhairav.

   [22]C Arabic scale: C-Db-E-F-G-Ab-B

   The double harmonic scale is arrived at by either:
     * raising the seventh of the [23]Phrygian dominant scale
     * raising the seventh and third of the [24]Phrygian mode
     * lowering the sixth and second of a [25]major scale
     * combining the lower half of phrygian dominant with the upper half
       of harmonic minor^[26][1]

   It is referred to as the "double harmonic" scale because it contains
   two augmented seconds/minor thirds, while the harmonic scale contains
   only that between the sixth and seventh degrees:

   [27]Double harmonic scale on C: augmented second between degrees 2-3
   and 6-7

   The double harmonic scale is uncommonly used in [28]Western music, as
   it does not closely follow any of the basic [29]musical modes, nor is
   it easily derived from them. It also does not easily fit into common
   Western [30]chord progressions such as the [31]authentic cadence.
   However, the Arabic scale (in the key of E) was used in [32]Nikolas
   Roubanis's "[33]Misirlou" and the Arabic scale is sometimes used in
   [34]heavy metal [35]guitar solos^[[36]citation needed]. It is popular
   in India^[37][2].

[[42]edit] Modes

   Like most hepatonic (seven pitches in the octave) scales, the double
   harmonic scale has a mode for each of its individual scale degrees. The
   most commonly known of these modes is the 4th mode, the [43]Hungarian
   gypsy scale, most similar to the [44]harmonic minor scale with a raised
   4th degree.

   The second mode is somewhat of a hybrid between the [45]blues scale
   (contains a #4) and [46]bebop scale (both b7 and 7) but there is no 6.

[[47]edit] Relationship to Phrygian major (Jewish scale)

   The nearest other existing scale to the double harmonic scale is the
   [48]Phrygian dominant scale. The double harmonic scale may be made from
   a Phrygian dominant scale by sharpening its 7th degree.
