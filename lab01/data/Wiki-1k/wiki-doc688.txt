Ealdorman

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

     (Redirected from [6]Ealdormanry)
   Jump to: [7]navigation, [8]search

   An ealdorman (from Old English ealdorman, lit. "elder man") is the term
   used for a high-ranking royal official and prior magistrate of an
   [9]Anglo-Saxon [10]shire from about the ninth century to the time of
   [11]King Cnut. The term ealdorman was rendered in [12]Latin as [13]dux
   in early [14]West Saxon charters, and as [15]prfectus (which
   confusingly, is also the equivalent of gerefa, modern [16]reeve, from
   which [17]sheriff or [18]shire reeve). In the Life of [19]King Alfred
   by the Welsh bishop [20]Asser, the Latin equivalent is comes.^[21][1]
   As the chief magistrate of a shire ([22]county) in [23]Anglo-Saxon
   [24]England, he commanded the army of the shire(s) and districts under
   his control on behalf of the king.

[[33]edit] Appointment

   They were appointees of the king and were originally mostly from the
   ancient and powerful families, but later were often chosen from among
   the king's comites (plural of [34]comes, lit. "companion") and many,
   especially in the early Danish period, were new to high office. The
   office was not hereditary, but there are several examples of
   tenth-century ealdormen whose sons became ealdormen (if not always of
   the same district), such as [35]thelstan Half-King and [36]thelweard
   the Chronicler.

[[37]edit] Earls

   Towards the end of the tenth century, the term ealdorman gradually
   disappeared as it gave way to eorl, probably under the influence of the
   Danish term jarl, which evolved into modern English [38]earl. The
   analogous term is sometimes [39]count, from the [40]French [41]comte,
   derived from the Latin comes. The ealdormen can be thought of as the
   early English earls, for their ealdormanries (singular ealdormanry,
   same meaning as [42]earldom) eventually became the great earldoms of
   Anglo-Danish and Anglo-Norman England.

   An ealdormancy was an Anglo-Saxon governing body over several shires,
   made up of more than one ealdorman.

[[43]edit] Aldermen

   Although earls may be regarded as the successors of ealdormen, the word
   ealdorman itself did not disappear and survives in modern times as
   [44]alderman. This term, however, developed distinct meanings which
   have little to do with ealdormen.

[[46]edit] Historical

     * [47]lfhere, ealdorman of Mercia (d. 983)
     * [48]lfhelm, ealdorman of southern Northumbria (d. c. 1006)

     * [51]Byrhtnoth, ealdorman of Essex (d. 991)
     * [52]Eadric Streona, ealdorman of the Saxon Mercians (d. 1017)

     * [54]Uhtred of Bebbanburg, hero of [55]The Saxon Stories, a series
