East Siberian Sea

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

     (Redirected from [6]Eastern Siberian Sea)
   Jump to: [7]navigation, [8]search
   Map of the East Siberian Sea.
   Satellite photo of the New Siberian Islands, with the Laptev Sea on the
   left and part of the East Siberian Sea shown on the right.

   The East Siberian Sea ([9]Russian: - ) is a [10]marginal sea in the
   [11]Arctic Ocean. It is located between the [12]Arctic Cape in the
   North, the coast of [13]Siberia in the South, the [14]New Siberian
   Islands in the West and [15]Cape Billings, close to [16]Gytkhelen,
   [17]Chukotka, and [18]Wrangel Island in the [19]East. This sea is
   bordering on the [20]Laptev Sea in the West and the [21]Chukchi Sea in
   the East.

   Since it is open towards the Arctic Ocean in the north, the main gulfs
   of the East Siberian Sea, like the [22]Kolyma Bay, the [23]Kolyma Gulf
   and the [24]Chauunskaya Bay, are all located in its southern limits.

   The area of the sea is 361,000 square miles. 70% of it is no deeper
   than 50 m, the deepest point being 155 m. The coast is mostly flat in
   the West (up to the mouth of the [25]Kolyma), and mountainous in the
   East. Average air temperatures are 0C to 2C (4C in the South) in the
   summer, reaching -30C in the winter.

   There are no islands in the middle of the East Siberian Sea, but there
   are a few islands and island groups in its coastal waters, like
   [26]Ayon Island and the [27]Medvyezhi island group.

   Owing to its extreme northerly location the East Siberian Sea is most
   of the time covered with [28]ice. The eastern coasts have some elevated
   terrain and hills, but the western coasts are mostly low-lying and
   covered with tundra, marshes and multitude of small lakes.

   Among the rivers flowing into the East Siberian Sea, the [29]Indigirka,
   the [30]Alazeya, The [31]Ujandina, the [32]Chukochya River, the
   [33]Kolyma, the [34]Rauchua, the [35]Chaun, and the [36]Pegtymel are
   the most important.

[[37]edit] History

   This sea was navigated by [38]Russian sea-farers, moving from one river
   mouth to another in their [39]kochs as early as the 17th century. In
   1648 [40]Semyon Dezhnev and Fedot Alekseev sailed the coast of the East
   Siberian Sea from the Kolyma to river [41]Anadyr in the [42]Bering Sea.
   Systematic exploration and mapping of the sea and its coasts was
   carried out by a series of expeditions in 1735-42, 1820-24, 1822, 1909,
   1911-14.

   The principal ports are [43]Pevek and [44]Logashkino. The latter was
   abandoned after navigation along the [45]Northern Sea Route declined in
   the latter half of the 20th century. After the breakup of the
   [46]Soviet Union commercial navigation in the Arctic went into decline.
   Nowadays more or less regular shipping is to be found only between
   Pevek and [47]Vladivostok. Ports in the northern Siberian coast located
   between [48]Dudinka and Pevek see next to no shipping at all.
