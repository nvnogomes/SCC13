Dover (film)

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

   Jump to: [6]navigation, [7]search

   Dover was a short 1943 film produced by the British [8]Ministry of
   Information. It concerns the town of [9]Dover, the most likely
   "frontline" in any potential German invasion and how it had persevered
   since 1940.

   The film opens with a look back at the circumstances of mid-1940, how
   the British had arrived there after [10]Dunkirk, the continuous air
   raids during the [11]battle of Britain, and the stoicism of the people
   as they prepared to "die with their boots on" when the invasion came.
   Now, two years later, Dover is still the front line, but not for
   defence, for offence. [12]RAF planes control the skies and more Allied
   sorties are flown over German targets than German raids on Britain. The
   people have stoically and bravely gone on with their normal lives,
   while helping the war effort; most of the men are in the forces, while
   the women man the anti-aircraft guns. The film ends with the narrator
   promising that someday soon, barges will leave Britain's shore to
   liberate Europe.
