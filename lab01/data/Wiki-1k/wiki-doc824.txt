Clyde Bernhardt

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

     (Redirected from [6]Ed Barron)
   Jump to: [7]navigation, [8]search

   Clyde Bernhardt (July 11, 1905, [9]Gold Hill, North Carolina - May 20,
   1986, [10]Newark, New Jersey) was an American [11]jazz trombonist.

   Bernhardt was born in [12]North Carolina but raised in [13]Harrisburg,
   [14]Pennsylvania. He started playing trombone at age 17, and in the
   1920s played with a variety of lesser-known ensembles, such as [15]Bill
   Eady's Ellwood Syncopators, [16]Tillie Vennie, [17]Odie Cromwell's
   Wolverine Syncopators, [18]Charlie Grear's Original Midnite Ramblers,
   the [19]Richard Cheatham Orchestra, the [20]Whitman Sisters, [21]Honey
   Brown's Orchestra, and [22]Ray Parker. He worked with [23]King Oliver
   in 1931, and through the middle of the decade did stints with [24]Alex
   Hill, [25]The Alabamians, [26]Billy Fowler, [27]Ira Coffey's
   Walkathonians, and [28]Vernon Andrade.

   In 1937 he joined [29]Edgar Hayes's orchestra, remaining there through
   1942, then worked with [30]Jay McShann, [31]Cecil Scott, [32]Luis
   Russell, [33]Leonard Feather, [34]Pete Johnson, [35]Wynonie Harris,
   [36]Claude Hopkins, and [37]Paul and [38]Dud Bascomb. He led his own
   ensemble, called the Blue Blazers, before returning to play with
   Russell from 1948-51. He recorded as a leader between 1946 and 1953,
   and on some of the recordings he sings under the [39]pseudonym Ed
   Barron.

   From 1952 to 1970 he played part-time with [40]Joe Garland's Society
   Orchestra, also working outside of music during this time. Following
   this he led the [41]Harlem Blues and Jazz Band between 1972 and 1979;
   his sidemen included [42]Doc Cheatham, [43]Charlie Holmes, [44]Happy
   Caldwell, [45]Tommy Benford, and [46]Miss Rhapsody. Bernhardt's heath
   began to fail in 1979, and he gave up leadership of the Harlem Blues
   and Jazz Band but played in [47]Barry Martyn's Legends of Jazz until
   his death in 1986. Shortly before his death he published an
   [48]autobiography co-written with [49]Sheldon Harris entitled I
   Remember.
