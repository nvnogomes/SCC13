   Droxidopa (L-threo DOPS, L-DOPS) is a synthetic [27]amino acid
   precursor of the [28]neurotransmitter and [29]hormone
   [30]norepinephrine ([31]noradrenalin).

[[39]edit] History

   Droxidopa was developed by Sumitomo Pharmaceuticals for treatment of
   neurogenic [40]orthostatic hypotension associated with various
   disorders including [41]Multiple System Atrophy, [42]Familial Amyloid
   Polyneuropathy, [43]hemodialysis induced hypotension and
   [44]Parkinson's Disease . The drug has been approved in Japan in these
   indications since 1989. Following a merger with Dainippon
   Pharmaceuticals in 2006, Dainippon Sumitomo Pharma licensed the drug to
   Chelsea Therapeutics to develop and market the drug worldwide except in
   Japan, Korea, China and Taiwan.

   Chelsea obtained Orphan Drug Status for droxidopa in the US for
   symptomatic neurogenic orthostatic hypotension associated with
   [45]Parkinsons Disease, [46]Pure Autonomic Failure and [47]Multiple
   System Atrophy and is currently in phase III trials.

[[48]edit] Therapeutic Use

   Droxidopa is a [49]prodrug of [50]norepinephrine used to increase the
   levels of norepinephrine in the body. It is metabolized by [51]aromatic
   L-amino acid decarboxylase. Patients with neurogenic orthostatic
   hypotension have depleted levels of norepinephrine which leads to
   decreased blood pressure upon orthostatic challenge.^[52][1] Droxidopa
   works by increasing the levels of peripheral norepinephrine thus
   enabling the body to maintain blood pressure during standing.

   Droxidopa can also cross the [53]blood-brain barrier where it is
   converted to norepinephrine.^[54][2] Increased levels of norepinephrine
   in the [55]central nervous system (CNS) may be beneficial to patients
   in a wide range of indications. Droxidopa can be coupled with a
   peripheral [56]DOPA decarboxylase inhibitor ([57]Carbidopa or
   [58]benserazide) to increase CNS norepinephrine levels while
   maintaining peripheral levels.

[[59]edit] Potential Indications

     * Neurogenic orthostatic hypotension
     * [60]Intradialytic hypotension (IDH)
     * Hypotension associated with [61]Fibromyalgia and [62]Chronic
       Fatigue Syndrome^[63][3]

[[64]edit] Adverse Effects

   With close to 20 years on the market, droxidopa has proven to have very
   few side effects of which most are mild. Patients have reported
   increased blood pressure, [65]nausea and [66]headache.^[67][4]

[[68]edit] Clinical Trials

   Droxidopa is currently being studied in phase III trials in the US,
   Canada, Australia and throughout Europe. Provided successful completion
   of the trials, droxidopa could be approved for the symptomatic
   neurogenic orthostatic hypotension indication in 2009.^[69][5]

   A phase II trial in IDH is also underway.
