Doucet

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

   Jump to: [6]navigation, [7]search

   Doucet is a [8]French language [9]surname, especially popular in
   [10]Canada, the former area of [11]Acadia in particular (now [12]Nova
   Scotia, [13]New Brunswick, [14]Prince Edward Island, and parts of
   [15]Quebec and [16]New England.

   As a result of the [17]Great Expulsion in 1755 and later from Acadia,
   Doucets are also amongst the [18]Cajuns (an anglicization of
   "Acadiens") of [19]Louisiana.

   The first Doucet to reach North America is thought to be Major
   [20]Germain Doucet dit La Verdure, a French military officer at
   Port-Royal (now [21]Annapolis Royal) in 1632 who attempted to defend
   what is now Maine and Acadia from invasions from Boston in 1654. Many
   Doucets in North America trace their lineage to Germain Doucet.
   ^[22][1]
   Doucet family crest

   Noteworthy people with this surname include:
     * [23]Gerald Doucet, former [24]Progressive Conservative politician
       from Nova Scotia
     * [25]Germain Doucet (1595-unknown), called Sieur de la Verdure,
       [26]French commandant in [27]Maine and [28]Acadia
     * [29]Henri Lucien Doucet (1856 1895), French painter
     * [30]Jacques Doucet, sportscaster, longtime play-by-play announcer
       for the [31]Montreal Expos
     * [32]Jacques Doucet (1853-1929), [33]French fashion designer
     * [34]Julie Doucet, Canadian cartoonist
     * [35]Michael Doucet, [36]Canadian singer-songwriter and guitarist.
     * [37]Lyse Doucet, presenter and correspondent for both the [38]BBC
       World Service radio and [39]BBC World television.
     * [40]Michael Doucet, [41]Cajun fiddler, singer and songwriter who
       founded the Cajun band [42]BeauSoleil from [43]Lafayette,
       Louisiana.
     * [44]Roger Doucet, [45]Canadian, (1919-1981) singer, known as Mr.
       O'Canada, for his rendition of Canada's National Anthem
       [46]Montreal.

   A common variant in spelling is "[47]Doucette". Most Doucets in Canada
   pronounce their surname as "DOU-set" or "Dou-SET", rather than
   "Dou-SAY" as modern French might require. Some argue this pronuciation
   may derive from dialects of sixteenth century French such as was found
   in Brittany, a common origin of French-Canadian settlers; however
   Dou-SAY is the most popular pronunciation among Cajuns in Louisiana.
