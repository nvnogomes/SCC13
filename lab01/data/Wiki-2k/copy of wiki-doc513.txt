JordanChevalley decomposition

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

     (Redirected from [6]Dunford decomposition)
   Jump to: [7]navigation, [8]search

   In mathematics, the JordanChevalley decomposition, named after
   [9]Camille Jordan and [10]Claude Chevalley, also known as Dunford
   decomposition, named after [11]Nelson Dunford, expresses a [12]linear
   operator as the sum of its commuting [13]semisimple part and its
   [14]nilpotent parts. The multiplicative decomposition expresses an
   invertible operator as the product of its commuting semisimple and
   unipotent parts. The decomposition is important in the study of
   [15]algebraic groups. The decomposition is related to the [16]Jordan
   normal form.

[[22]edit] Linear operators

   Consider linear operators on a finite-dimensional [23]vector space. An
   operator is semisimple if the roots of its [24]minimal polynomial are
   all distinct (if the underlying field is [25]algebraically closed, this
   is the same as the requirement that the operator be
   [26]diagonalizable). An operator x is nilpotent if some power x^m of it
   is the zero operator. An operator x is unipotent if x1 is nilpotent.

   Now, let x be any operator. The JordanChevalley decomposition expresses
   x as a sum:

          x = x[ss] + x[n],

   where x[ss] is semisimple, x[n] is nilpotent, and x[ss] and x[n] are
   polynomials in x (in particular they commute). This decomposition is
   unique, in that if x = s + n with s semisimple, n nilpotent, and sn =
   ns, then s = x[ss] and n = x[n], ([27]Humphreys 1972, Prop. 4.2, p.
   17).

   If x is an invertible operator, then the JordanChevalley decomposition
   expresses x as a product:

          x = x[ss] x[u],

   where x[ss] is semisimple, x[u] is unipotent, and x[ss] and x[u] are
   polynomials in x (in particular they commute). This decomposition is
   unique, in that if x = s u with s semisimple, u unipotent, and su = us,
   then s = x[ss] and u = x[u].

   For endomorphisms of a finite dimensional vector space over an
   algebraically closed field, there is a simple description in terms of
   the [28]Jordan normal form. If x is in the Jordan normal form, then
   x[ss] is the matrix containing just the diagonal terms of x, x[n] is
   the matrix containing just the off-diagonal terms, and x[u] is the
   matrix with the diagonal entries replaced by 1s.

[[29]edit] Matrices

   The JordanChevalley decomposition is an abstract form of the [30]Jordan
   normal form of a matrix. The Jordan normal form J of a [31]complex
   matrix A satisfies A = MJM^1 for some invertible matrix M and J = D +
   N, where D is a [32]diagonal matrix and N is a matrix with the only
   nonzero terms in the first codiagonal, thus N is nilpotent. Then A =
   MDM^1 + MNM^1 is the additive JordanChevalley decomposition of A into a
   semisimple matrix and nilpotent matrix. Similarly, if A is invertible,
   then D is invertible and A = MDM^1M(I + D^1N)M^1 is the multiplicative
   JordanChevalley decomposition of A into a semisimple matrix and a
   unipotent matrix.

[[33]edit] Banach spaces

   Please help [34]improve this section by expanding it. Further
   information might be found on the [35]talk page. (January 2009)

   For operators on [36]Banach spaces, there is a decomposition known as
   the Dunford decomposition which generalizes the JordanChevalley
   decomposition, ([37]Dunford 1946).
