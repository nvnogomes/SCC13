   In [11]mathematics, a divisor of an [12]integer n, also called a factor
   of n, is an integer which evenly divides n without leaving a
   [13]remainder.

[[22]edit] Explanation

   For example, 7 is a divisor of 42 because 42/7 = 6. We also say 42 is
   divisible by 7 or 42 is a [23]multiple of 7 or 7 divides 42 or 7 is a
   factor of 42 and we usually write 7 | 42 (a [24]vertical bar between
   the two numbers). For example, the positive divisors of 42 are 1, 2, 3,
   6, 7, 14, 21, 42.

   In general, we say m|n (read: m divides n) for non-zero integers m and
   n [25]iff there exists an integer k such that n = km. Thus, divisors
   can be [26]negative as well as positive, although often we restrict our
   attention to positive divisors. (For example, there are six divisors of
   four, 1, 2, 4, 1, 2, 4, but one would usually mention only the positive
   ones, 1, 2, and 4.)

   1 and 1 divide (are divisors of) every integer, every integer (and its
   negation) is a divisor of itself, and every integer is a divisor of 0,
   except by convention 0 itself (see also [27]division by zero). Numbers
   divisible by 2 are called [28]even and numbers not divisible by 2 are
   called [29]odd.

   A divisor of n that is not 1, 1, n or n (which are trivial divisors) is
   known as a non-trivial divisor; numbers with non-trivial divisors are
   known as [30]composite numbers, while [31]prime numbers have no
   non-trivial divisors.

   The name comes from the [32]arithmetic operation of [33]division: if
   a/b = c then a is the [34]dividend, b the divisor, and c the
   [35]quotient.

   There are [36]properties which allow one to recognize certain divisors
   of a number from the number's digits.

   For example, the [37]set A = { 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, 30, 60

[[40]edit] Further notions and facts

   [41]Sister project The [42]Wikibook [43]The Book of Mathematical Proofs
   has a page on the topic of
   [44]Proofs of properties of divisibility

   Some elementary rules:
     * If a | b and a | c, then a | (b + c), in fact, a | (mb + nc) for
       all integers m, n.
     * If a | b and b | c, then a | c. ([45]transitive relation)
     * If a | b and b | a, then a = b or a = b.

   The following property is important:
     * If a | bc, and [46]gcd(a,b) = 1, then a | c. ([47]Euclid's lemma)

   A positive divisor of n which is different from n is called a proper
   divisor (or [48]aliquot part) of n. (A number which does not evenly
   divide n, but leaves a remainder, is called an [49]aliquant part of n.)

   An integer n > 1 whose only proper divisor is 1 is called a [50]prime
   number. Equivalently, one would say that a prime number is one which
   has exactly two factors: 1 and itself.

   Any positive divisor of n is a product of [51]prime divisors of f
   raised to some power. This is a consequence of the [52]Fundamental
   theorem of arithmetic.

   If a number equals the sum of its proper divisors, it is said to be a
   [53]perfect number. Numbers less than the sum of their proper divisors
   are said to be [54]abundant; while numbers greater than that sum are
   said to be [55]deficient.

   The total number of positive divisors of n is a [56]multiplicative
   function d(n) (e.g. d(42) = 8 = 222 = d(2)d(3)d(7)). The sum of the
   positive divisors of n is another multiplicative function (n) (e.g.
   (42) = 96 = 348 = (2)(3)(7)). Both of these functions are examples of
   [57]divisor functions.

   If the [58]prime factorization of n is given by

          n = p_1^{\nu_1} \, p_2^{\nu_2} \cdots p_k^{\nu_k}

   then the number of positive divisors of n is

          d(n) = (\nu_1 + 1) (\nu_2 + 1) \cdots (\nu_k + 1),

   and each of the divisors has the form

          p_1^{\mu_1} \, p_2^{\mu_2} \cdots p_k^{\mu_k}

   where 0 \le \mu_i \le \nu_i for each 0 \le i \le k.

   One can show ^[59][1] that

          d(1)+d(2)+ \cdots +d(n) = n \ln n + (2 \gamma -1) n +
          O(\sqrt{n}).

   One interpretation of this result is that a randomly chosen positive
   integer n has an expected number of divisors of about lnn.

[[60]edit] Divisibility of numbers

   The relation of divisibility turns the set N of [61]non-negative
   integers into a [62]partially ordered set, in fact into a [63]complete
   distributive lattice. The largest element of this lattice is 0 and the
   smallest one is 1. The meet operation ^ is given by the [64]greatest
   common divisor and the join operation v by the [65]least common
   multiple. This lattice is isomorphic to the [66]dual of the [67]lattice
   of subgroups of the infinite [68]cyclic group [69]Z.

[[70]edit] Generalization

   One can talk about the concept of divisibility in any [71]integral
   domain. Please see that article for the definitions in that setting.
