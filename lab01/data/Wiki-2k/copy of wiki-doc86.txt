   Alvin Pleasant Delaney Carter (December 15, 1891 - November 7, 1960),
   best known as A.P. Carter, was an [27]American [28]musician and
   founding member of [29]The Carter Family group, one of the most notable
   acts in the history of [30]Country music.

[[36]edit] Biography

[[37]edit] Life

   A.P. Carter was born to Robert C. Carter and Mollie Arvelle Bays in
   [38]Maces Spring, [39]Virginia (then known as Poor Valley). A.P. was
   sometimes called "Doc."

   On June 18, 1915, he married [40]Sara Dougherty and they had three
   children: Gladys (Millard), Janette (Jett), and Joe. In 1927, he formed
   the [41]Carter Family band together with his wife. They were joined by
   Sara's cousin, [42]Maybelle, who was married to A.P.'s brother, Ezra
   Carter, and they together formed the first commercial rural Country
   music group. Carter was known for travelling extensively throughout the
   country and collecting and blending songs, particularly from
   [43]Appalachian musicians. Some of the songs became so closely
   identified with A. P. Carter that he has been popularly, but
   mistakenly, credited with writing them. For example, "Keep on the Sunny
   Side of Life" was published in 1901 with the words being credited to
   Ada Blenkhorn and the music credited to Howard Entwisle, ^[44][1] and
   "The Meeting in the Air" has been published giving credit for music and
   words to I. G. Martin.^[45][2]

   A.P. and Sarah separated in 1932,^[46][3] largely a result of Sara
   having an affair with A.P.'s cousin, due to A.P.'s long absences from
   home in search of new musical ideas. They officially divorced in
   1939.^[47][4] The band remained together for several years afterwards,
   but broke up in 1943. While Maybelle and her daughters continued to
   tour as The Carter Family, A.P. left the music business to run a
   [48]general store in Virginia.^[49][5] In 1952, A.P. reformed The
   Carter Family with Sarah and some of their grown children; the reunion
   lasted until 1956.^[50][6]

   A.P. Carter died in [51]Kingsport, [52]Tennessee on November 7, 1960 at
   the age of 68.^[53][7]^[54][8] He was buried in the Mount Vernon
   Methodist Church Cemetery in [55]Hiltons, Virginia.^[56][9]

[[57]edit] Legacy

   Despite dying in relative obscurity, A. P. Carter was inducted into the
   [58]Nashville Songwriters Hall of Fame in 1970. In 1993, his image
   appeared on a [59]U.S. postage stamp honoring the Carter Family. In
   2001 he was inducted posthumously into the [60]International Bluegrass
   Music Hall of Honor.

   [61]PBS aired a one-hour show on A.P. Carter and the Carter Family on
   [62]American Experience.

   In recent years, The [63]Barter Theatre in [64]Abingdon, Virginia has
   performed a play based on A.P.'s life called "Keep On The Sunny Side".

   On her 2008 [65]album [66]All I Intended to Be, [67]Emmylou Harris
   includes the song "How She Could Sing the Wildwood Flower", co-written
   with [68]Kate and Anna McGarrigle, about the relationship between A.P.
   and Sara, inspired by a [69]documentary that the three of them saw on
   [70]television.

   A.P. Carter is interred in the Mt. Vernon United Methodist Church
   graveyard in [71]Hiltons, Virginia.^[72][10]
