   E is the fifth letter in the [40]Latin alphabet. Its name in
   [41]English is spelled e (pronounced [42]/i/), plural ees (pronounced
   [43]/iz/)(rare).^[44][1] The letter E is the most commonly used letter
   in the [45]Czech,^[46][2] [47]Danish,^[48][2] [49]Dutch,^[50][2]
   [51]English,^[52][3] [53]French,^[54][4] [55]German,^[56][5]
   [57]Hungarian,^[58][2] [59]Latin,^[60][2] [61]Norwegian,^[62][2]
   [63]Spanish,^[64][6] and [65]Swedish languages.^[66][2]

[[72]edit] History

   E is derived from the [76]Greek letter [77]epsilon which is much the
   same in appearance (, ) and function. In [78]etymology, the [79]Semitic
   [80]h probably first represented a praying or calling human figure
   ([81]hillul jubilation), and was probably based on a similar
   [82]Egyptian hieroglyph that was pronounced and used quite differently.
   In Semitic, the letter represented /h/ (and /e/ in foreign words), in
   Greek h became (Epsilon) with the value /e/. [83]Etruscans and
   [84]Romans followed this usage. Arising from the [85]Great Vowel Shift,
   [86]English usage is rather different, namely /i/ (derived from /e/ in
   "me" or "bee") whereas other words like "bed" are closer to Latin and
   other languages in usage.

[[87]edit] Usage

   Like other [88]Latin vowels, E came in a long and a short variety.
   Originally, the only difference was in length but later on, short e
   represented //. In other languages that use the [89]letter E or e, it
   represents various other phonetic values, sometimes with accents to
   indicate contrasts (e ).

   [90]Digraphs starting with E are common in many languages to indicate
   [91]diphthongs and [92]monophthongs, such as EA or EE for /i/ or /e/ in
   English, EI for /a/ in [93]German, or EU for // in [94]French or // in
   German.

   At the end of a word, E is very often silent in English ([95]silent e),
   where old noun [96]inflections have been dropped, although even when
   silent at the end of a word it often causes vowels in the word to be
   pronounced as [97]diphthongs, conventionally called [98]long vowels
   (compare as a noun rat and as a verb rate).

   The letter 'E' is the most common (or highest [99]frequency) letter in
   the [100]English language (starting off the typographer's phrase
   [101]ETAOIN SHRDLU) and many other related [102]languages, which has
   implications in both [103]cryptography and [104]data compression. This
   makes it a difficult and popular letter to use when writing
   [105]lipograms. [106]Ernest Vincent Wright's [107]Gadsby (1939), is
   considered a "dreadful" novel, and that "at least part of Wright's
   narrative difficulties were caused by language restrictions imposed by
   the lack of E."^[108][7] Both [109]Georges Perec's novel [110]A Void
   (La Disparition) (1969) and its English translation by Gilbert Adair
   omit the letter E and are considered better works.^[111][8]

[[112]edit] Codes for computing

   Alternative representations of E
   [113]NATO phonetic [114]Morse code
   Echo
   [115]Signal flag [116]Flag semaphore [117]Braille

   In [118]Unicode the [119]capital E is codepoint U+0045 and the
   [120]lower case e is U+0065.

   The [121]ASCII code for capital E is 69 and for lower case e is 101; or
   in [122]binary 01000101 and 01100101, respectively.

   The [123]EBCDIC code for capital E is 197 and for lowercase e is 133.

   The [124]numeric character references in [125]HTML and [126]XML are
   "&#69;" and "&#101;" for upper and lower case, respectively.
