Conrotatory and disrotatory

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

     (Redirected from [6]Disrotatory)
   Jump to: [7]navigation, [8]search

   In a conrotatory mode of an [9]electrocyclic reaction (a class of
   [10]organic [11]chemical reactions) the [12]substituents located at the
   termini of a [13]conjugated [14]double bond system move in the same
   ([15]clockwise or counter clockwise) direction during ring opening or
   ring closure. In a disrotatory mode, they move in opposite directions.

   A simple [16]arrow-pushing analysis fails to explain the quantitative
   conversion of [17]trans-cis-trans-2,4,6-octatriene to
   [18]cis-dimethylcyclohexadiene (top of figure). However, a detailed
   look at the orbital mechanics behind the process reveals that the
   orbital symmetry of the octatriene's HOMO requires for the end pi
   orbitals to move in opposite directions to form the correct symmetry
   found in the sigma bond.
   [19]Disrotatory ring closing reaction

   This [20]stereospecificity is found in thermal rearrangements of all
   conjugated systems containing 4n + 2 [21]pi electron and is based on
   preservation of [22]orbital symmetry in the [23]highest occupied
   molecular orbital. Systems containing 4n pi electrons show the opposite
   [24]conrotatory mode, as do photoinduced rearrangements of 4n + 2 pi
   electrons. Photoinduced rearrangements of 4n electron systems follow
   the disrotatory rule.

   The [25]Woodward-Hoffmann rules summarize the above different
   reactions.
