Dobson ozone spectrophotometer

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

     (Redirected from [6]Dobsonmeter)
   Jump to: [7]navigation, [8]search

   The Dobson spectrophotometer, also known as Dobsonmeter, is the
   earliest instrument used to measure atmospheric [9]ozone. It was
   developed in 1924 by [10]Gordon Dobson.

   Dobson spectrophotometers can be used to measure both total column
   ozone and profiles of ozone in the [11]atmosphere.

   Total ozone measurements are made by measuring the relative intensities
   of selected pairs of [12]ultraviolet (UV) wavelengths. One wavelength
   (305 nm) is strongly absorbed by ozone and the other (325 nm) is not
   ^[13][1]. The sources of [14]light used may vary. Beside the direct
   [15]sun light, the light from the clear sky, [16]moon or [17]stars may
   be used.

   The vertical distribution of ozone is derived using the Umkehr method.
   This method relies on the intensities of reflected, rather than direct,
   [18]UV light. Ozone distribution is derived from the change in the
   ratio of two UV frequencies with time as the sun sets. An Umkehr
   measurement takes about three hours, and provides data up to an
   altitude of 48 km, with the most accurate information for altitudes
   above 30 km.

   The Dobson method has its drawbacks. It is strongly affected by
   [19]aerosols and [20]pollutants in the atmosphere, because they also
   absorb some of the light at the same wavelength. Measurements are made
   over a small area. Today this method is often used to calibrate data
   obtained by other methods, including satellites.

   Some modernized versions of Dobson spectrophotometer exist and continue
   to provide data, e.g. the [21]Brewer Spectrophotometer.

   About 120 Dobsonmeters have been made, mostly by R&J Beck of London.
   Today about 50 remain in use. The most famous ones are probably Nos. 37
   and 51 with which [22]Joe Farman of the [23]British Antarctic Survey
   discovered the [24]Ozone Hole in 1984. The oldest instrument still in
   use is No.8 located at the roof of the [25]Norwegian Polar Institute at
   [26]Ny lesund, [27]Svalbard.
