   addition to appearing in the saga's movies and TV series, several
   [12]LucasArts games depict the player as a starfighter pilot.

[[35]edit] A-wing

          Main article: [36]A-wing

   A-wings appear at the climactic [37]Battle of Endor in [38]Star Wars
   Episode VI: Return of the Jedi.

[[39]edit] ARC-170 starfighter

   ARC-170 starfighters appear in the opening sequence of [40]Star Wars
   Episode III: Revenge of the Sith. The fighter's name stems from "ART
   170", the file name of the art that established the ship's appearance
   -- deliberately reminiscent of the [41]X-wing.^[42][1]

[[43]edit] B-wing

          Main article: [44]B-wing

   B-wings participate at the Battle of Endor in Return of the Jedi.

[[45]edit] Droid starfighter

   Automated Trade Federation droid starfighters appear in space battles
   in [46]Star Wars Episode I: The Phantom Menace and Revenge of the Sith.
   Original designs depicted the droid starfighter not as a droid itself,
   but instead piloted by a [47]battle droid.^[48][2]

[[49]edit] Droid tri-fighter

   A pair of droid tri-fighters

   Tri-fighters are part of the droid forces in the opening battle of
   Revenge of the Sith. Original plans to equip the fighters with boosters
   were abandoned when Lucas decided they were an unnecessary distraction
   in the already chaotic scene.^[50][3] Its curved braces are reminiscent
   of the Trade Federation's circular battleship.^[51][3]

[[52]edit] E-wing

   E-wings first appear in [53]Dark Horse Comics' [54]Dark Empire series.
   They are one of few Expanded Universe vehicles to be a part of
   [55]Micro Machines' Action Fleet line.^[56][4]

[[57]edit] General Grievous' starfighter

   [58]Obi-Wan Kenobi ([59]Ewan McGregor) escapes Utapau in Grievous'
   starfighter in Revenge of the Sith. The starfighter, "both sleek and
   aggressive", was one of few ships to be physically built for the
   production.^[60][5]

[[61]edit] Geonosian fanblade starfighter

   [62]Asajj Ventress flies the fanblade in the animated [63]star Wars:
   Clone Wars series. Just as Ventress' appearance was inspired by a
   [64]Samurai stance, the ship itself has Asian influences: its design is
   based on a [65]sensu folding fan.^[66][6]

[[67]edit] Geonosian starfighter

   Two Geonosian starfighters in the Battle of Geonosis

   Geonosian starfighters appear in the climactic Battle of Geonosis in
   [68]Star Wars Episode II: Attack of the Clones. The craft was set to
   appear in an air battle and a scene in which a [69]Republic attack
   gunship attack a Geonosian airstrip -- scenes that were ultimately
   cut.^[70][7]

[[71]edit] Hornet Interceptor

   Hornets are small starfighters built for sale on the [72]black market
   by the [73]Tenloss Syndicate.

[[74]edit] Jedi interceptor

          Main article: [75]Jedi starfighter

   Jedi interceptors appear in Revenge of the Sith.

[[76]edit] Jedi starfighter

          Main article: [77]Jedi starfighter

   Jedi starfighters appear in Attack of the Clones.

[[78]edit] Naboo N-1 starfighter

   The N-1 Starfighter first appeared during the space battle period of
   the Battle of Naboo in [79]Star Wars Episode I: The Phantom Menace
   where a [80]squadron of the fighters attacked the orbiting [81]Droid
   Control Ship. During the Battle of Naboo, [82]Anakin Skywalker
   ([83]Jake Lloyd) pilots a Naboo N-1 Starfighter into the Droid Control
   Ship's docking bay. N-1 Starfighters also appear in the beginning of
   [84]Star Wars Episode II: Attack of the Clones escorting senator
   [85]Padm Amidala's ([86]Natalie Portman) [87]Naboo Royal Cruiser upon
   its arrival to Coruscant. The N-1 appears at the end of the DVD version
   of [88]Star Wars Episode VI: Return of the Jedi. This ship is available
   to the player in the [89]Nintendo 64 and [90]PC video game [91]Star
   Wars: Battle for Naboo. The starfighter is also an unlockable vehicle
   in the [92]Star Wars: Rogue Squadron series.

   The N-1 initially had the same angular design as craft in the original
   Star Wars trilogy; only in later designs did it take on a streamlined
   appearance.^[93][8] A life-size model of a Naboo Starfighter hangs
   suspended from the ceiling of the Blue Wing at the [94]Boston Museum of
   Science, in Boston, Massachusetts.^[95][9] This 1:1 scale model was
   unveiled at the museum in April 2004, as a prelude to the [96]Star
   Wars: Where Science Meets Imagination exhibit.^[97][9] The model was
   previously used as a prop during the filming of The Phantom
   Menace.^[98][9]

[[99]edit] P-38 starfighter

   The P-38 appears in Revenge of the Sith. Although featured in a full
   two-page spread in the Revenge of the Sith Incredible Cross-Sections
   book, the fighter's role was significantly reduced in the final film;
   it is visible mostly as a background craft.^[100][10]

[[101]edit] TIE fighter (and variants)

          Main article: [102]TIE fighter

   TIE fighters -- and variants such as TIE interceptors and TIE bombers
   -- appear throughout the original trilogy.

[[103]edit] V-19 Torrent starfighter

   The V-19 appears in the Clone Wars animated series. LucasArts' art
   director Chris Williams and concept artist Greg Knight developed the
   craft as a faster, more maneuverable counterpart to the Republic
   assault gunship.^[104][11] The ship's engines are based on those of an
   [105]F4U Corsair.^[106][11]

[[107]edit] V-wing

   V-wings appear in the Revenge of the Sith and are playable craft in
   Battlefront II. Its design combines elements of the original trilogy's
   [108]A-wing and [109]TIE starfighters.^[110][12] It is the first
   prequel trilogy vehicle to use a letter to identify its
   class.^[111][12]

[[112]edit] X-wing

          Main article: [113]X-wing

   X-wing fighters appear in all three of the original Star Wars films,
   and main character [114]Luke Skywalker ([115]Mark Hamill) flies one at
   the climactic Battle of Yavin in A New Hope.

[[116]edit] Y-wing

          Main article: [117]Y-wing

   Y-wings appear in all three of the original Star Wars films.

[[118]edit] Z-95 Headhunter

   Z-95s are first described in the [119]Brian Daley's 1979 novella Han
   Solo at Star's End, where it is described as having [120]swing-wings
   and a bubble cockpit.^[121][13] Later Expanded Universe material, based
   on an early [122]Joe Johnston X-wing sketch, depict the Z-95 as a
   precursor to the X-wing.^[123][13]
