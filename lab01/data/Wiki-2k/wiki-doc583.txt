Dynkin system

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

     (Redirected from [6]Dynkin lemma)
   Jump to: [7]navigation, [8]search

   A Dynkin system, named in honor of the Russian mathematician [9]Eugene
   Dynkin, is a [10]collection of [11]subsets of another universal [12]set
   satisfying some specific rules. They are also referred to as -systems.
   These set families have applications in [13]measure theory.

[[14]edit] Definitions

   Let be a [15]nonempty set, and let D be a collection of subsets of ,
   i.e. D is a subset of the [16]power set of . Then D is a Dynkin system
   if
     * the set itself is in D
     * D is [17]closed under [18]relative complementation, i.e. A,B\in D
       and A \subseteq B implies B \setminus A \in D
     * D is closed under the [19]countable [20]union of increasing
       [21]sequences, i.e. A_n\in D and A_n \subseteq A_{n+1},\ n \ge 1
       implies \cup_{n=1}^{\infty}A_n\in D .

   D is a -system if
     * the set itself is in D
     * D is closed under [22]complementation, i.e. A\in D implies A^c\in D
     * D is closed under [23]disjoint countable unions, i.e. A_n\in D,
       n\geq1 with A_i\cap A_j=\emptyset for all i\neq j implies
       \cup_{n=1}^\infty A_n\in D .

   It can be shown that these two definitions are [24]logically
   equivalent, so that Dynkin systems are -systems and vice versa.

   A Dynkin system which is also a [25]-system is a [26]-algebra.

   Given any collection \mathcal{J} of subsets of , there exists a unique
   Dynkin system denoted D\{\mathcal J\} which is minimal with respect to
   containing \mathcal J . That is, if \tilde D is any Dynkin system
   containing \mathcal J , then D\{\mathcal J\}\subseteq\tilde D .
   D\{\mathcal J\} is called the Dynkin system generated by \mathcal{J} .
   Note D\{\emptyset\}=\{\emptyset,\Omega\} . For another example, let =
   {1,2,3,4} and \mathcal J=\{1\} ; then D\{\mathcal
   J\}=\{\emptyset,\{1\},\{2,3,4\},\Omega\} .

[[27]edit] Dynkin's - Theorem

   If P is a -system and D is a Dynkin system with P\subseteq D , then
   \sigma\{P\}\subseteq D . In other words, the -algebra generated by P is
   contained in D.

   One application of Dynkin's - theorem is the uniqueness of the
   [28]Lebesgue measure:

   Let (, B, ) be the [29]unit interval [0,1] with the Lebesgue measure on
   [30]Borel sets. Let be another [31]measure on satisfying [(a,b)] = b -
   a, and let D be the family of sets such that [D] = [D]. Let I = {
   (a,b),[a,b),(a,b],[a,b] : 0 < a b < 1 }, and observe that I is closed
   under finite intersections, that I D, and that B is the -algebra
   generated by I. One easily shows D satisfies the above conditions for a
   Dynkin-system. From Dynkin's lemma it follows that D is in fact all of
   B, which is equivalent to showing that the Lebesgue measure is unique.
