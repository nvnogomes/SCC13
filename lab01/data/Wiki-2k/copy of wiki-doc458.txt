Duchess of Marlborough (Faberg egg)

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

     (Redirected from [6]Duchess of Marlborough (Faberge egg))
   Jump to: [7]navigation, [8]search

   The [9]Duchess of Marlborough egg (also known as the Pink Serpent egg
   ^[10][1]) is a [11]jewelled [12]enameled [13]Easter egg made by
   [14]Michael Perchin under the supervision of the [15]Russian jeweller
   [16]Peter Carl Faberg in 1902 ^[17][2].

   The Duchess of Marlborough egg is the only large Faberg Easter egg to
   have been commissioned by an American, and is inspired by a [18]Louis
   XVI clock with a revolving dial. It is similar to the 1887 [19]Blue
   Serpent Clock egg. ^[20][2]

[[25]edit] History

   The egg was made for [26]Consuelo Vanderbilt, who became The Duchess of
   Marlborough when she married [27]Charles Spencer-Churchill, 9th Duke of
   Marlborough in 1895. ^[28][2]

   In 1902 the Duchess and her husband travelled to Russia, where they
   dined with [29]Nicholas II of Russia, and visited his mother, the
   Dowager Empress [30]Maria Feodorovna at the [31]Anichkov Palace. At
   this visit the Duchess would have almost certainly seen the Dowager
   Empress' large collection of Faberg, perhaps inspiring her to order
   this egg. ^[32][2]

   This egg is believed to have cost over 5,000 [33]rubles. ^[34][2]

   After her divorce from the Duke of Marlborough, Vanderbilt donated the
   Duchess of Marlborough egg to a charity auction in 1926. The egg was
   bought by [35]Ganna Walska, the second wife of [36]Harold Fowler
   McCormick, chairman of the [37]International Harvester Company of
   Chicago. ^[38][2] At the 1965 [39]Parke-Bernet auction of her property,
   it was bought by [40]Malcolm Forbes. It was the first Faberg Easter egg
   that Forbes purchased. ^[41][2]

   In 2004 it was sold as part of Forbes Collection to [42]Viktor
   Vekselberg. Vekselberg purchased some nine Imperial eggs, as part of
   the collection, for almost $100 million. ^[43][3]
