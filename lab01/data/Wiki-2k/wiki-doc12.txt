Distal splenorenal shunt procedure

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

   Jump to: [6]navigation, [7]search

   In [8]medicine, a distal splenorenal shunt procedure (DSRS), also
   splenorenal shunt procedure and Warren shunt,^[9][1] is a [10]surgical
   procedure in which the [11]distal [12]splenic vein (a part of the
   [13]portal venous system) is attached to the left [14]renal vein (a
   part of the [15]systemic venous system). It is used to treat [16]portal
   hypertension and its main complication ([17]esophageal
   varices).^[18][2]

[[23]edit] Splenopancreatic and gastric disconnection (SPGD)

   DSRS is typically done with splenopancreatic and gastric disconnection
   (ligation of the [24]gastric veins and [25]pancreatic veins (that drain
   into the [26]portal vein) and complete detachment of the [27]splenic
   vein from the [28]portal venous system), as it improves the
   outcome.^[29][3]

[[30]edit] Comparison to TIPS

   Survival with a [31]transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt
   (TIPS) versus a DSRS is thought to be approximately
   similar,^[32][4]^[33][5] but still an area of intensive
   research.^[34][6]^[35][7]

   Both TIPS and DSRS lead to decreased rates of [36]variceal bleeding at
   the expense of [37]hepatic encephalopathy; however, TIPS appears to
   have more shunt dysfunction and lead to more encephalopathy and
   bleeds.^[38][5] DSRS appears to be more cost effective than
   [39]TIPS.^[40][8]
