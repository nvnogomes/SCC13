Drungarios

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

   Jump to: [6]navigation, [7]search

   A droungarios, also spelled drungarios ([8]Greek: , [9]Latin:
   drungarius) or, in its English form, drungary, was a [10]military rank
   of the late [11]Roman and [12]Byzantine Empires.

[[19]edit] History and use

[[20]edit] Late Roman and Byzantine army

   The term drungus is first attested in Latin in the later fourth
   century. It derives from Gaulish *dhrungho (see Old Irish drong; Old
   Breton drogn or drog), meaning tribe, group, throng or crowd. An
   alternative Germanic etymology cited by some historians originates in
   seventeenth-century guesswork which has been rejected by the
   overwhelming majority of philologists.^[21][1] The earliest usage of
   drungus in Latin is non-technical and similarly signifies a generic
   band or troop, which Vegetius equates to Latin globus.^[22][2] The term
   first occurs in Greek as droungos (), with the same meaning, in the
   early fifth century.^[23][3] In the late sixth century, the Emperor
   [24]Maurice applies droungos to a specific tactical deployment, usually
   of cavalry, characterised as a compact non-linear grouping suited to
   outflanking tactics, ambushes and irregular operations. He is the first
   author to employ the cognate adverb droungisti (), with the sense of
   "in group formation" or "small-group tactics".^[25][4] Maurice also
   occasionally employs droungos as a generic expression for larger
   "groupings" or "formations" of troops, though in this sense he refers
   only to a "division" (meros) and never to a "brigade" (moira) with
   which droungos became associated in later sources.^[26][5]

   The term droungarios () is not documented before the early seventh
   century but might have been used as an informal or unofficial
   designation before that date. The office and the corresponding unit
   appear to have initially referred to [27]ad hoc arrangements, but
   during the early seventh century these were formalized, like much of
   the Eastern Roman army's rank structure.^[28][6] In the new
   military-administrative [29]theme system, every major division, called
   a thema (), was further divided into tourmai while each tourma was
   divided into [30]moirai () or droungoi, which in turn were composed of
   several [31]banda. Thus each moira or droungos was the analogue of a
   modern regiment or brigade, initially ca. 1000 men strong (and hence
   also referred to as a [32]chiliarchia), although on occasion it could
   rise to 3000 men, and Emperor [33]Leo VI the Wise is recorded as having
   established droungoi of only 400 men for the new smaller
   themes.^[34][7]

   The commander of the elite Vigla regiment (one of the [35]tagmata) also
   bore the rank of droungarios, and was later raised to the rank of megas
   droungarios ts vigls ( , "Grand Drungary of the Vigla"). In the 11th
   century, this office also assumed significant judicial
   responsibilities, since its holder became the president of the imperial
   court of the Vlon, housed at the "Covered Hippodrome" adjoining the
   [36]imperial palace.^[37][8] In the Komnenian period, its holders, men
   like Andronikos Kamateros, were amongst the emperor's senior
   aides.^[38][9]

   In the [39]Palaiologan period, this office was the tenth in the overall
   hierarchy, according to the list of Pseudo-Kodinos, and on campaign,
   its holder was responsible for setting the watch around the imperial
   camp.^[40][10]

[[41]edit] Byzantine Navy

   The rank of droungarios was also used in the [42]Byzantine navy to
   designate its [43]admirals. The droungarios tou basilikou plmou ( ) was
   the commander of the central Imperial Fleet, based at and around
   [44]Constantinople, while the provincial ("thematic") fleets were also
   commanded by a droungarios (although it was later replaced by the more
   exalted rank of [45]strategos), to whose title was added the name of
   the thema under his command, e.g. droungarios tn Kibyrraitn ( , the
   admiral of the Cibyrrhaeotic Theme). The droungarios of the Imperial
   Fleet was later raised to the rank of megas droungarios [tou plmou]
   ("Grand Drungary [of the Fleet]") acting as commander in chief of the
   entire navy, until he was replaced in this task by the [46]megas doux
   in the 1090s. The office of megas droungarios of the fleet continued to
   exist, in a subordinate position, until the Empire's fall. The variant
   rank of droungarokoms () also existed, signifying a count (koms) in
   command of a squadron of warships.

   The rank of droungarios was one of the lowest military ranks that
   carried an accompanying [47]court title, ranging from hypatos to
   vesttr.^[48][11]
