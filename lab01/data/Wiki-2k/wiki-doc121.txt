   The maras (Dolichotis) are a [20]genus of the [21]cavy family. They are
   the sole representatives of the subfamily Dolichotinae. These large
   relatives of [22]guinea pigs are common in the [23]Patagonian steppes
   of Argentina but live in other areas of South America as well such as
   Paraguay. Maras are the fourth largest [24]rodent in the world, after
   [25]capybaras, [26]porcupines, and [27]beavers, reaching about 45 cm
   (18 in) in height.

   There are two species of maras recognised, the [28]Patagonian Mara
   (Dolichotis patagonum), and the [29]Chacoan Mara (Dolichotis
   salinicola). They are known as the pampas hare.

   Patagonian Maras are often kept in [30]zoos or as pets and is also
   known as "Patagonian cavy" or "Patagonian hare". They can be quite
   social with humans if raised with human interaction from a young age,
   though in the wild they avoid humans. Maras may even change their
   habits from coming out in day to becoming nocturnal, simply to avoid
   social interaction.

   Maras mate for life, and may have from 1 to 3 offspring each year. Mara
   babies are very well developed, and can start grazing within 24 hours.

[[35]edit] Physical description & movement

   Maras have stocky bodies, three sharp-clawed digits on the hind feet,
   and four digits of the forefeet. Maras have been described as
   resembling long-legged [36]rabbits. Patagonian Maras can run at speeds
   up to 29 km (18 mi) per hour. Maras can weigh over 11 [37]kg (25
   [38]lb) in adulthood.

   Most Maras have a brown head and body, a dark almost black rump with a
   white fringe around the base, and a white belly.

   Maras may walk normally, hop in a rabbit-like fashion, gallop, or
   bounce on all fours. They have been known to leap up to six feet (about
   2 meters).
