   Frank Herbert's Dune is a three-part [28]miniseries written and
   directed by [29]John Harrison and based on [30]Frank Herbert's 1965
   novel [31]Dune.

   Produced by New Amsterdam Entertainment, Blixa Film Produktion and
   [32]Hallmark Entertainment Distribution, the series was first broadcast
   in the [33]United States on December 3, 2000 on the [34]Sci Fi Channel.
   It was later released on [35]DVD in 2001, with a [36]director's cut
   appearing in 2002.

   A 2003 [37]sequel miniseries called [38]Frank Herbert's Children of
   Dune continued the story, adapting the second and third novels in the
   series (1969's [39]Dune Messiah and its 1976 sequel [40]Children of
   Dune). As of 2004, both miniseries were two of the three highest-rated
   programs ever to be broadcast on the Sci Fi Channel.^[41][1]

   Frank Herbert's Dune won two [42]Emmy Awards in 2001 for
   [43]Cinematography and [44]Visual effects in a miniseries/movie, as
   well as being nominated for a third Emmy for [45]Sound editing.

   The miniseries was shot in [46]Univisium (2.00:1) [47]aspect ratio,
   although it was broadcast in 1.78:1.

[[53]edit] Adaptation

   Director John Harrison has described his film adaptation as a "faithful
   interpretation"^[54][2], in which any changes he made served to suggest
   what Herbert had explained subtly or not at all. The miniseries
   introduces elements not found in Herbert's novel, but according to the
   director, these serve to elaborate rather than to edit. Harrison's
   television adaptation and [55]David Lynch's 1984 [56]film adaptation
   both depart from the content of the novel, but Harrison's treatment of
   Herbert's thematic and philosophical content is usually compared
   favorably to the 1984 film.^[[57]citation needed]

[[106]edit] Departures from the novel

   Alec Newman portrays [107]Paul Atreides as emotionally immature at the
   start of the film, whereas in Herbert's novel, Paul is mature beyond
   his years throughout the narrative. In the book, Paul was 15-18 years
   old, but Harrison aged the character to 18-22 in order to increase the
   quality of the acting for this crucial role.^[108][4]

   The miniseries invents an extensive subplot for [109]Princess Irulan, a
   character who plays little part in the plot of the first novel.
   Harrison felt the need to expand Irulan's role because she played such
   an important part in later books, and [110]epigraphs from her later
   writings opened each chapter of Dune.^[111][5] Additionally, the
   character gave him a window into [112]House Corrino.^[113][2] Besides
   the final scene, the only one of Irulan's appearances based on an
   actual excerpt from the novel is her visit to [114]Feyd-Rautha.
   However, in the book it is a different [115]Bene Gesserit, [116]Margot
   Fenring, who visits the [117]Harkonnen heir, on assignment from the
   Bene Gesserit to "preserve the bloodline" by retrieving his genetic
   material (through conception) for their [118]breeding program. The
   miniseries does not suggest this as Irulan's motive.

   The miniseries implements several stylistic changes. For example,
   Herbert's [119]ornithopters are described as birdlike, but though the
   miniseries presents ornithopters with flapping wings, they more closely
   resemble insects. According to the novel, extensive use of [120]melange
   tints the [121]sclera, [122]cornea and [123]iris of the user to a dark
   shade of blue, called "blue-in-blue" or "the Eyes of Ibad".^[124][6] In
   the miniseries, the eyes of the spice-addicted have a [125]fluorescent,
   "glowing" appearance. In the case of Jessica, before she undergoes the
   rite to become a [126]Fremen [127]Reverend Mother by changing the
   [128]Water of Life, her eyes are normal; immediately after the rite,
   they glow blue.
