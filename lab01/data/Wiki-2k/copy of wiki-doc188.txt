   Donald Lee Hollowell (1917 - 2004) was a [12]civil rights [13]attorney
   in the [14]State of Georgia.

[[21]edit] Early life and Education

   Donald Hollowell was born and raised in [22]Wichita, Kansas, and earned
   a high school diploma while serving six years in the [23]U.S. Army's
   [24]10th Cavalry Regiment (the original [25]Buffalo Soldier regiment).
   Although in [26]Kansas, Hollowell did not encounter the racist [27]Jim
   Crow laws of the South, he faced blatant [28]racism and
   [29]discrimination while serving in the [30]U.S. Army during [31]World
   War II. Hollowell recounted that army officials relegated him to eating
   in the kitchen, sleeping in quarters adjacent to prisoners, and
   patronizing Jim Crow canteens. Hollowells experiences with [32]racial
   segregation and discrimination and his involvement with the
   [33]Southern Negro Youth Congress after the war inspired him to pursue
   the study of law to help in the fight for social justice. ^[34][1] In
   1947, Hollowell graduated magna cum laude from [35]Lane College in
   [36]Tennessee, and he earned his law degree from [37]Loyola University
   Chicago School of Law in 1951.

[[38]edit] Legal career

   In 1952, Hollowell set up a law practice in [39]Atlanta, Georgia, where
   he began to play a major role in the burgeoning [40]civil rights
   struggle.^[41][1]

   Hollowell became well known for fighting [42]racial segregation in the
   [43]State of Georgia. Hollowell sued the [44]University of Georgia,
   charging the institution with racist admission policies. The suit ended
   in 1961 with a federal court order demanding the admission of two
   [45]African American students, [46]Charlayne Hunter and [47]Hamilton E.
   Holmes.

   In 1960, Hollowell and co-counsel [48]Horace Ward won a victory in the
   [49]Georgia Court of Appeals which secured the release of [50]Rev. Dr.
   Martin Luther King Jr. from the [51]Georgia State Prison. In another
   case, Hollowell and members of his firm prevented the [52]electrocution
   of a 15-year-old black youth from [53]Monticello, Georgia, five days
   before the scheduled execution. Hollowell and civil rights champion C.
   B. King also defended Dr. King and hundreds of civil rights activists
   in the historic civil rights campaign in [54]Albany, Georgia known as
   the [55]Albany Movement.

   In 1966, President [56]Lyndon B. Johnson appointed Hollowell as
   regional director of the [57]Equal Employment Opportunity Commission
   (EEOC), a government agency that monitors workplace discrimination.
   This appointment made Hollowell the first black regional director of a
   major federal agency.^[58][1] Hollowell remained with the EEOC for
   nearly 20 years. Hollowell also served as president of the [59]Voter
   Education Project, where he helped increase the number of
   [60]African-American voters from 3 million to 5.5 million.^[61][1]

   In 2002, the [62]University of Georgia awarded Hollowell its honorary
   [63]Doctor of Laws degree.^[64][2]

   Hollowell passed away on [65]December 27, [66]2004 of [67]heart
   failure. He was 87 years old.

   To honor him, the City of [68]Atlanta renamed [69]Bankhead Highway
   ([70]U.S. 78) in his honor; [71]Emory University named a professorship
   in his honor, as well.

[[72]edit] Personal

   Hollowell was a dedicated member of [73]Kappa Alpha Psi Fraternity,
   Inc. In 1968, he received the Fraternity's highest honor, the Laurel
   Wreath Award, for his work in [74]civil rights.

   Hollowell was married to Louise T. Hollowell, a [75]magna cum laude
   graduate of [76]Morris Brown College and a distinguished Professor of
   English (Emeritus) at [77]Morris Brown. In 1997, Louise Hollowell and
   Martin Lehfeldt authored a book titled The Sacred Call: A Tribute to
   Donald L. HollowellCivil Rights Champion, which chronicles Hollowell's
   service and achievements. The book also tells the love story of Donald
   and Louise Hollowell, who celebrated their 61st wedding anniversary in
   2004.^[78][1]
