   Drew Saunders Days III, an [15]American [16]lawyer, served as
   [17]United States Solicitor General from 1993 to 1996 under
   [18]President [19]Bill Clinton. He also served as the first [20]African
   American [21]Assistant Attorney General for the [22]Civil Rights
   Division in the [23]Carter administration from 1977 to 1980.^[24][1]

   He is currently the Alfred M. Rankin Professor of Law at [25]Yale Law
   School, assuming that post in 1992, and joining the Yale Law faculty in
   1981.^[26][2] Since 1997, he has also headed the Supreme Court and
   appellate practice at [27]Morrison & Foerster LLP and is currently
   [28]of counsel at the firm's [29]Washington, D.C. office. He has been
   admitted to practice law before the [30]United States Supreme Court,
   and in the states of [31]Illinois and [32]New York.^[33][3]

[[38]edit] Education and early career

   He graduated from [39]New Rochelle High School in [40]New Rochelle, New
   York, before going on to earn an undergraduate degree from [41]Hamilton
   College, and a law degree from [42]Yale Law School. Upon graduation
   from law school, he briefly practiced law in [43]Chicago, Illinois,
   before becoming a [44]Peace Corps volunteer in [45]Honduras.

   Returning to the United States in 1969, he became first assistant
   counsel at the [46]NAACP Legal Defense and Educational Fund in [47]New
   York City. Days worked at LDF for eight years litigating a range of
   civil rights cases.^[48][3]

[[49]edit] Appointments and professorship

   In 1977, President Jimmy Carter nominated him to serve as the Assistant
   Attorney General for Civil Rights in the United States Department of
   Justice. His tenure was marked by an aggressive enforcement of the
   nations civil rights laws.^[50][3]

   Mr. Days served in the Department of Justice until 1981 when he joined
   the faculty of the Yale Law School. In 1988 he founded the [51]Orville
   H. Schell Jr. Center for Human Rights at Yale Law School and served as
   its director until 1993.^[52][2]

   In 1993 he was nominated by President Clinton to serve as Solicitor
   General in the Department of Justice. In that position, he was
   responsible for representing the positions and interests of the United
   States in arguments before the [53]Supreme Court.^[54][2]

[[55]edit] Private practice

   After leaving the Clinton administration, he returned to Yale Law
   School and private practice. Mr. Days currently continues to be
   involved in national and international efforts to resolve many of our
   most critical social and economic issues including Hurricane Katrina,
   poverty alleviation, the environment, and juvenile justice.^[56][3]
