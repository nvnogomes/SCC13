Edith Rigby

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

   Jump to: [6]navigation, [7]search

   Edith Rigby (1872 - 1948) was an English [8]suffragette. She was a
   contemporary of [9]Christabel and [10]Sylvia Pankhurst.

[[16]edit] Biography

   Born Edith Rayner in 1872 in [17]Preston, [18]Lancashire, she was one
   of seven children of Dr. Charles Rayner and was educated at [19]Penrhos
   College in [20]North Wales.^[21][1] She married Dr. Charles Rigby and
   lived with him in [22]Winckley Square in Preston. From an early age she
   questioned the differences between working-class and middle-class women
   and after she was married she worked hard to improve the lives of women
   and girls working in local mills. In 1899, she founded St. Peter's
   school, which allowed these women to meet and continue their education
   which otherwise would have stopped after the age of 11.^[23][2]^[24][3]
   At home, she was critical of her neighbours' treatment of their
   servants. The Rigbys had servants themselves, but allowed them certain
   unconventional freedoms such as being able to eat in the dining-room
   and not having to wear uniforms.^[25][4]

[[26]edit] Activism

   In 1907 she formed the Preston branch of the [27]Women's Social and
   Political Union.^[28][5] Also this year, she took part in a march to
   the [29]Houses of Parliament in [30]London with [31]Christabel and
   [32]Sylvia Pankhurst. 57 women, including Rigby, were arrested and
   sentenced to a month in prison.^[33][3] During this time (and her
   subsequent sentences seven in total) Rigby took part in [34]hunger
   strikes and was subjected to [35]force-feeding.^[36][3]^[37][4] Her
   activism included planting a bomb in the [38]Liverpool Corn Exchange,
   pouring acid on a local [39]golf course and committing [40]arson at
   [41]Blackburn Rovers Football Club.^[42][4] In 1913, Rigby was
   responsible for the burning of [43]Lord Leverhulme's [44]bungalow at
   [45]Rivington Pike.^[46][3] Afterwards she said:
   I want to ask Sir William Leverhulme whether he thinks his property on
   Rivington Pike is more valuable as one of his superfluous houses
   occasionally opened to people, or as a beacon lighted to King and
   Country to see here are some intolerable grievances for women.^[47][4]

[[48]edit] Later life

   According to Elizabeth Ashworth in Champion Lancastrians, Rigby was the
   first lady to ride a bicycle in Preston.^[49][3] During the [50]World
   War I, she bought a cottage near Preston and used it to produce food
   for the [51]war effort.^[52][4] With short hair and wearing men's
   clothes, she grew fruit and vegetables and kept animals and [53]bees,
   following the teachings of [54]Rudolf Steiner.^[55][3] She had a happy
   marriage with her husband, who retired and lived with her at her
   cottage.^[56][3] They adopted a son called Sandy.^[57][3] In the 1920s
   Rigby was a founding member and the president of the [58]Hutton and
   [59]Howick [60]Women's Institute.^[61][4]

   In 1926, shortly after the death of her husband, Rigby moved to
   [62]North Wales.^[63][3] She continued to follow Steiner's work,
   forming an "[64]Anthrophosophical Circle" of her own, and visiting one
   of his schools in [65]New York.^[66][3] Into old age she enjoyed a
   healthy lifestyle, bathing in the sea, [67]fell walking and
   [68]meditating in the early hours of every morning.^[69][3] She
   eventually suffered from [70]Parkinson's disease and died in 1948 near
   [71]Llandudno, [72]Wales.^[73][3]
