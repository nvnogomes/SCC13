   Edwin Thompson Jaynes ([23]Waterloo, Iowa, [24]July 5, [25]1922 [26]St.
   Louis, Missouri, [27]April 30, [28]1998) was Wayman Crow Distinguished
   Professor of Physics at [29]Washington University in [30]St. Louis. He
   wrote extensively on [31]statistical mechanics and on foundations of
   [32]probability and [33]statistical inference, initiating in 1957 the
   [34]MaxEnt interpretation of thermodynamics^[35][1]^[36][2], as being a
   particular application of more general [37]Bayesian/[38]information
   theory techniques (although he argued this was already implicit in the
   works of [39]Gibbs). He was one of the first to interpret
   [40]probability theory as an extension of [41]Aristotelian logic.

   In 1963, together with [42]Fred Cummings, he modelized the evolution of
   a [43]two-level atom in an electromagnetic field, in a fully quantized
   way. This model is known as the [44]Jaynes-Cummings model.

   A particular focus of his work was the construction of logical
   principles for assigning prior probability distributions; see the
   [45]principle of maximum entropy, the principle of transformation
   groups^[46][3]^[47][4] and [48]Laplace's [49]principle of indifference.

   His last book, Probability Theory: The Logic of Science gathers various
   threads of modern thinking about [50]Bayesian probability and
   [51]statistical inference, and contrasts the advantages of Bayesian
   techniques with the results of other approaches. It was published
   posthumously in 2003 by [52]Cambridge University Press from an
   incomplete manuscript by editor [53]Larry Bretthorst.
