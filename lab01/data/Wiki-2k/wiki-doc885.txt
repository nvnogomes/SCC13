Edlingham Castle

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

   Jump to: [6]navigation, [7]search
   The ruins of Edlingham Castle
   [8]Sister project [9]Wikimedia Commons has media related to:
   [10]Edlingham Castle

   Edlingham Castle is a small [11]castle ruin, having [12]Scheduled
   Ancient Monument and [13]Grade I listed building status, in the care of
   [14]English Heritage, in a valley to the west of [15]Alnwick,
   [16]Northumberland, [17]England ([18]grid reference [19]NU116092).
   [20]Edlingham itself is little more than a hamlet with a church
   alongside the castle.

   The ruins are mostly laid low though much of the [21]solar tower still
   stands despite an impressive crack running several stories down to
   ground level. The foundations and part of the walls of the hall house,
   [22]gatehouse, [23]barbican and other courtyard buildings are still
   visible, most dating from the 16th century.

   The castle - more properly a fortified [24]manor house typical of many
   [25]medieval houses in the [26]North of England - guards one of the few
   approaches to [27]Alnwick through the hills to its west. Its
   fortifications were increased in response to the [28]border warfare
   which raged between [29]England and [30]Scotland in the period from
   about 1300 to 1600.

[[31]edit] History

   By 1174, a manor house at the location was in the possession of a John
   of Edlingham. In 1294, a descendant, Walter of Edlingham sold it to
   William de Felton, who strengthened it by building strong ramparts and
   a gatehouse, fortifying the main hall and adding other buildings inside
   a courtyard. In 1396 Elizabeth de Felton inherited it, marrying Sir
   Edmund Hastings, who added a strong solar tower. Their descendants
   occupied the castle and estate until 1514; it was then leased to George
   Swinburne of Capheaton, whose family held it until the 1700s.
   View of Edlingham Castle ruins from the gatehouse

   During this time it gradually fell into disrepair, with most of the
   buildings dismantled to build nearby farmhouses in the 1660s, but
   leaving the solar tower intact. In 1978 the [32]Department for the
   Environment acquired the site and conducted extensive
   [33]archaeological excavations, prior to which rubble filled the solar
   tower to a height of three metres.

   The site is now in the care of [34]English Heritage and is easily
   accessible from the nearby church of [35]St. John the Baptist. There is
   an interpretation board on-site, while more detailed leaflets are
   available from the church for a small donation.
