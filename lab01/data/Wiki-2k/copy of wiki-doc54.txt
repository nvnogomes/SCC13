   Tanis (), the [13]Greek name of ancient Djanet (modern n al-aar), is a
   city in the north-eastern [14]Nile delta of [15]Egypt. It lies on the
   Tanitic branch of the [16]Nile (now silted up).

[[22]edit] History

   Tanis was founded in the late [23]Twentieth dynasty, and became the
   northern capital of Egypt during the following [24]Twenty-first
   dynasty. It was the home city of [25]Smendes, founder of the 21st
   dynasty. During the [26]Twenty-second dynasty Tanis remained as Egypt's
   political capital (though there were sometimes rival dynasties located
   elsewhere in [27]Upper Egypt). It was an important commercial and
   strategic city until it was threatened with inundation by [28]Lake
   Manzala in the 6th century AD, when it was finally abandoned. The
   refugees founded the nearby city of [29]Tennis.

[[30]edit] Ruins

   The ruins of Tanis

   There are ruins of a number of temples, including the chief temple
   dedicated to [31]Amun, and a very important royal [32]necropolis of the
   [33]Third Intermediate Period (which contains the only known intact
   royal Pharaonic burials - the tomb of [34]Tutankhamun having been
   entered in antiquity). Many of the stones used to build the various
   temples at Tanis came from the old Ramesside town of [35]Qantir
   (ancient [36]Pi-Ramesses/Per-Ramesses), which caused many former
   generations of [37]Egyptologists to believe that Tanis was, in fact,
   [38]Per-Ramesses. However the burials of three dynasty 21 and dynasty
   22 pharaohs--[39]Psusennes I, [40]Amenemope and [41]Shoshenq II,
   survived the depradations of tomb robbers throughout antiquity. They
   were discovered intact in 1939 and 1940 by [42]Pierre Montet and proved
   to contain a large catalogue of gold, jewellry, lapis lazuli and other
   precious stones including the funerary masks of these kings.

   The chief deities of Tanis were [43]Amun, his consort, [44]Mut, and
   their child [45]Khonsu, forming the Tanite triad. This triad was,
   however, identical to that of [46]Thebes, leading many scholars to
   speak of Tanis as the "northern Thebes".

[[47]edit] In popular culture

   In the film [48]Raiders of the Lost Ark, Tanis was said to be the
   resting place of the [49]Ark of the Covenant which was hidden in a
   secret chamber. Tanis was inaccurately depicted having been destroyed
   in a [50]sand storm and buried until 1936 when it was discovered by a
   German expedition outside of [51]Cairo.
