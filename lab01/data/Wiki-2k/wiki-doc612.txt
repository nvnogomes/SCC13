Glutamate transporter

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

     (Redirected from [6]EAAT5)
   Jump to: [7]navigation, [8]search

   Excitatory Amino Acid Transporters (EAAT), formerly known as Glutamate
   transporters, belong to the family of [9]neurotransmitter transporters.
   They serve to terminate the excitatory neurotransmitter signal by
   removal (uptake) of [10]glutamate from the neuronal [11]synapse into
   [12]Glia cells.

   In details, the EAATs are membrane-bound pumps that resemble [13]ion
   channels.^[14][1] These transporters play the important role of
   regulating concentrations of glutamate in the [15]extracellular space,
   keeping it at low levels.^[16][2] After glutamate is released as the
   result of an [17]action potential, glutamate transporters quickly
   remove it from the extracellular space to keep its levels low, thereby
   terminating the synaptic transmission.^[18][1]^[19][3]

   Without the activity of glutamate transporters, glutamate would build
   up and kill cells in a process called [20]excitotoxicity, in which
   excessive amounts of glutamate acts as a toxin to neurons by triggering
   a number of [21]biochemical cascades. The activity of glutamate
   transporters also allows glutamate to be recycled for repeated
   release.^[22][4]

   Glutamate transporters are also present in many other tissues such as
   bone and [23]testes.

[[31]edit] Classes

   There are two classes of glutamate transporters, those that are
   dependent on an [32]electrochemical gradient of [33]sodium ions (the
   EAATs) and those that are not (VGluTs).^[34][5] Some sodium independent
   transporters such as the cystein-glutamate antiporter are localised to
   plasma membrane of cells whilst others the are called vesicular
   transporters. Na^+-dependent transporters are actually also dependent
   on K^+ concentrations, and so are also known as 'sodium and potassium
   coupled glutamate transporters' or, in humans, 'excitatory amino acid
   transporters' (EAATs).^[35][6] Some Na^+-dependent transporters have
   also been called 'high-affinity transporters', though their glutamate
   affinity actually varies widely.^[36][6]

   [37]mitochondria also possess mechanisms for taking up glutamate that
   are quite distinct from membrane glutamate transporters.^[38][6]

[[39]edit] EAATs

   In humans (as well as in rodents), five subtypes have been identified
   and named EAAT1-5 ([40]SLC1A3, [41]SLC1A2, [42]SLC1A1, [43]SLC1A6,
   [44]SLC1A7). Subtypes EAAT1-3 are found in membranes of glial cells
   ([45]astrocytes, [46]microglia, and [47]oligodendrocytes) as well as in
   [48]endothelial cells, whereas EAAT4 is located on neurons.^[49][5].
   Finally, EAAT5 is only found in the retina where it is principally
   localised to photoreceptors and bipolar neurons in the retina.^[50][7].
   In rodents, the [51]orthologs for EAAT1-3 are named [52]GLAST, GLT1,
   and EAAC1, respectively^[53][3], whereas the acronyms EAAT4 and EAAT5
   are conserved.

   When glutamate is taken up into Glia cells by the EAATs, it is not
   reused directly but converted to glutamine and stored vesicles.
   Subsequently these vesicle are released from Glia cells and glutamine
   transported back into the presynaptic neuron, converted back into
   glutamate, and store into vesicles by action of the
   VGLUTs.^[54][8]^[55][3] This process is named the
   [56]glutamate-glutamine cycle.

   The Glia transporters - in particularly the various splice variants of
   GLT-1 (EAAT2) - play the largest role (90%) in regulating extracellular
   glutamate concentration.^[57][9]

[[58]edit] VGluTs

   three types of vesicular glutamate transporters, VGLUTs 13.
   ([59]SLC17A7, [60]SLC17A6, [61]SLC17A8)^[62][3] In addition to these
   transportersVesicular glutamate transporters pack the neurotransmitter
   into [63]synaptic vesicles so that they can be released into the
   synapse. VGLUTs are dependent on a [64]proton gradient that they create
   by hydrolysing [65]adenosine triphosphate (ATP). VGLUTs have only
   between one hundredth and one thousandth the affinity for glutamate
   that EAATs have.^[66][3] Also unlike EAATs, they do not appear to
   transport aspartate.

[[67]edit] Pathology

   Overactivity of glutamate transporters may result in inadequate
   synaptic glutamate and may be involved in [68]schizophrenia and other
   mental illnesses.^[69][1]

   During injury processes such as [70]ischemia and [71]traumatic brain
   injury, the action of glutamate transporters may fail, leading to toxic
   buildup of glutamate. In fact, their activity may also actually be
   reversed due to inadequate amounts of [72]adenosine triphosphate to
   power [73]ATPase pumps, resulting in the loss of the
   [74]electrochemical ion gradient. Since the direction of glutamate
   transport depends on the ion gradient, these transporters release
   glutamate instead of removing it, which results in neurotoxicity due to
   overactivation of [75]glutamate receptors.^[76][10]

   Loss of the Na^+-dependent glutamate transporter EAAT2 is suspected to
   be associated with [77]neurodegenerative diseases such as
   [78]Alzheimer's disease, [79]Huntington's disease, and
   [80]ALSparkinsonism dementia complex.^[81][11] Also, degeneration of
   [82]motor neurons in the disease [83]amyotrophic lateral sclerosis has
   been linked to loss of EAAT2 from patients' [84]brains and [85]spinal
   cords.^[86][11]
